The Mystery Job.

A work analysis investigation. You are a compensation consultant. A role exists, work is being performed, but the title has been removed from organisational records. Your task is not to guess the title. Your task is to understand the work.

The Pedagogical Intent

Compensation professionals make decisions about work, not about titles. Yet most learners arrive in a compensation course assuming the title is the unit of analysis. This simulation is designed to disrupt that assumption — to make visible the discipline of looking at evidence first, naming the work, and only then considering what the role might be called.

The simulation does not test memory. It develops a way of seeing. By the end, learners should understand why work analysis is the foundation of compensation management — and why job descriptions, job evaluation, internal pay structure, and pay equity all rest on it.

Phase 03 · Work Analysis

Organise the evidence.

Compensation professionals analyse work along eight dimensions. Tap any evidence item in the pool to select it, then tap a category to place it. The same evidence can be reconsidered and moved — work analysis is an iterative discipline, not a one-shot sort.

What you are doing: Each evidence item points to one or more dimensions of work. Some are clear; some require judgement. There is no single correct sort — but there are defensible sorts.

How to think: Ask of each piece of evidence — does this tell me what is done (task), what must be achieved (responsibility), what gets produced (output), who is interacted with (stakeholder), what must be known (knowledge), what skill is exercised (skill), what is used (tool), or what is decided (decision)?

Phase 04 · Job Reconstruction

The work analysis report.

Convert your analysis into a formal report. This is the artefact you would deliver to a client — every field below is what a compensation team would need before designing a job description, evaluating the job, or setting its pay.

Note on the job title field: the title is the last thing you write, not the first. Resist the temptation to start there. Write the work first. The title will follow — and in many cases, more than one title will fit.

i Proposed Job Title
What would you call this role, based on the work you have seen? Choose a title that reflects the work, not your guess at the industry standard.
ii Job Purpose Statement
In two or three sentences — what does this role exist to do? What is the result the organisation expects?
iii Top Five Responsibilities
The recurring outcomes this role is accountable for. One per line. Use active verbs.
iv Key Outputs
The tangible artefacts this role produces — reports, dashboards, decisions, recommendations.
v Key Stakeholders
Who this role interacts with — upward, sideways, and external.
vi Knowledge Requirements
What must the person know to perform the role well? Domain knowledge, frameworks, organisational context.
vii Skills Required
What the person must be able to do — analytical, technical, interpersonal.
viii Tools Used
Systems, platforms, software, and methods the role uses daily or weekly.
ix Decisions Influenced
What organisational decisions does this role shape or inform? This is often the truest signal of role value.
x Confidence Level
How confident are you in the analysis above? Be honest — overconfidence in work analysis leads to overpriced or underpriced jobs.

Phase 05 · Expert Debrief

The expert's view.

Your analysis has been compared against an expert work analysis model. The comparison is not about matching wording — it is about conceptual coverage across the eight dimensions of work.

Read the expert model carefully. The reveal that follows is the central learning of this simulation.
